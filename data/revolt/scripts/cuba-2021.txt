A: Cuba: 11J. A blackout, a protest video, and the broadest unrest since 1959.
B: On 11 July 2021, in the town of San Antonio de los Banos outside Havana, residents facing months of blackouts and shortages amid the pandemic and a collapsing economy took to the streets.
A: It did not come from nowhere. Cuba's economy contracted an estimated 11 percent in 2020, and residents faced rolling blackouts of eight hours or more alongside shortages of basic food and medicine.
B: Cuba remains a one-party state under the Communist Party, the only legal party under the constitution, with no direct multi-party elections for its top leadership.
A: Then the spark. Protests begin in San Antonio de los Banos and spread to dozens of towns and cities within hours, the widest single-day unrest since 1959.
B: Diaz-Canel calls on 'revolutionaries' to confront protesters. Security forces and mobilized party brigades move through protest areas overnight; internet access is cut nationwide.
A: The security forces held with the regime, which is usually decisive.
B: It was crushed. At least one protester death was confirmed by authorities in Havana; independent tallies of deaths and injuries during the crackdown remain incomplete.
A: The government's response, mass arrest, harsh sentencing and periodic internet shutdowns during later flashpoints, has kept protest episodic rather than sustained. Emigration, not confrontation, has been the dominant response since: hundreds of thousands of Cubans left in the years after 11J, one of the largest exoduses in the country's history.
